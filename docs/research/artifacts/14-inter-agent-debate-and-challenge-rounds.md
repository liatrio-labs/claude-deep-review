# How multi-agent systems resolve disagreements in code review

**Majority voting, not debate, drives most performance gains in multi-agent LLM systems** — and this finding fundamentally reshapes how challenge rounds should work in code review. The "Debate or Vote" paper (Choi et al., 2025) proves mathematically that debate induces a martingale over agent beliefs: expected correctness remains unchanged across rounds. Production systems like Anthropic's Code Review and Qodo 2.0 have quietly absorbed this lesson, favoring verification pipelines and centralized judge agents over adversarial debate. Yet the research also reveals that ensemble disagreement is a powerful signal — when agents disagree on a code finding, that disagreement itself carries information about the finding's importance and difficulty. The practical challenge is designing systems that harvest this signal without falling into sycophancy, false consensus, or wasted compute.

## The "Debate or Vote" result overturns multi-agent debate assumptions

The landmark paper by Choi, Zhu, and Li at the University of Wisconsin-Madison (arXiv:2508.17536, August 2025) ran a systematic comparison of multi-agent debate (MAD) against simple majority voting across **seven benchmarks**: Arithmetics, GSM8K, MMLU-Pro Medicine, CommonsenseQA, HellaSwag, Formal Logic, and HH-RLHF. Using Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Llama3.1-8B-Instruct as primary models, with validation on Qwen2.5-32B, the paper compared single agents, decentralized MAD (Du et al. style), sparse MAD, centralized MAD, and standalone majority voting.

The results were striking: **majority voting performance was essentially on par with the best MAD configuration** across all seven benchmarks, while both massively outperformed single agents. The paper's Theorem 2 provides a formal proof: under a Dirichlet-Compound-Multinomial model, debate induces a **martingale** over agents' beliefs in the correct answer, meaning the expected belief remains unchanged across debate rounds. Debate is a random walk, not a climb. Theorem 1 shows that majority voting success probability approaches 1 as agent count increases, even with small correctness margins — explaining why voting alone captures the gains.

This result spawned a productive follow-up literature. "Demystifying Multi-Agent Debate" (arXiv:2601.19921) proposes two mechanisms to **break the martingale**: diversity-aware initialization and confidence-modulated updates that create a submartingale (expected improvement). "Breaking the Martingale Curse" (arXiv:2603.06801) introduces AceMAD with peer prediction scoring, achieving **49.92% average accuracy with GPT-4o-mini** — a 74% relative improvement over decentralized MAD. The "Martingale Score" paper (arXiv:2512.02914) finds pervasive belief entrenchment (violations of the martingale property) across LLM reasoning, with violations correlating with accuracy drops.

The complementary "Voting or Consensus?" paper (Kaesberg et al., ACL Findings 2025) evaluated seven decision protocols across six tasks using Llama 3 8B and 70B models. **Voting protocols improved performance by 13.2% on reasoning tasks**, while consensus protocols improved by only 2.8% on knowledge tasks. A critical finding: increasing discussion rounds *before* voting actually **reduces** performance. Approval voting failed entirely in 59% of cases because agents agreed too readily. The companion MALLM framework (EMNLP 2025) provides 144+ configurations for systematically testing these protocols.

For code review — which combines reasoning (is this logic correct?) with knowledge (does this pattern have known vulnerabilities?) — these results suggest a **hybrid approach**: voting for logic-error detection, consensus-seeking for security and architectural concerns where domain knowledge matters more than majority opinion.

Du et al.'s foundational work ("Improving Factuality and Reasoning," ICML 2024) established the positive case for multi-agent debate using 3 ChatGPT agents over 2 rounds across six benchmarks. They demonstrated **5–10% absolute improvement** on GSM8K and MMLU over single-agent chain-of-thought, with debate particularly effective for factual accuracy in biography generation. Cross-model debate (ChatGPT + Bard) also worked, both agents being initially wrong but converging on correct answers through debate. However, subsequent research has substantially qualified these results — the gains appear to come primarily from the aggregation step, not the deliberation.

## Sycophancy corrupts multi-agent verification more than expected

The sycophancy problem in multi-agent systems is now well-documented through multiple independent studies and represents the single largest threat to challenge-round effectiveness. When agents share context, they tend to agree rather than critically evaluate — precisely the opposite of what a challenge mechanism needs.

"When Identity Skews Debate" (arXiv:2510.07517), from the same Wisconsin-Madison group behind "Debate or Vote," introduces the Identity Bias Coefficient (IBC) to quantify sycophancy versus self-bias. Of **20 evaluated configurations, 18 showed dominant sycophancy** (agents conforming to peers) versus only 2 showing self-bias (agents stubbornly maintaining positions). The paper proposes Response Anonymization — stripping agent identity labels — as a mitigation that shifts reasoning from identity-driven to content-driven evaluation.

"Talk Isn't Always Cheap" (arXiv:2509.05396) delivers perhaps the most alarming finding: debate can **actively degrade** performance. Stronger agents flip from correct to incorrect answers more often than weaker agents learn correct ones. In heterogeneous groups (mixing GPT-4, LLaMA, and Mistral on GSM8K and MMLU), agents converge on wrong answers together. The longer debate continues, the worse performance gets in many settings. The paper attributes this to RLHF post-training, which makes LLMs compliant with peer opinions even when those opinions are wrong.

CONSENSAGENT (Pitre et al., ACL Findings 2025) characterizes sycophancy as inflating computational costs by requiring additional debate rounds to reach decisions that end up being no better than the starting positions. Their solution — dynamic prompt refinement based on agent interactions — achieves state-of-the-art results across six benchmark datasets. "Peacemaker or Troublemaker" (arXiv:2509.23055) offers a nuanced framing: sycophancy isn't purely negative, since it enables agents to adopt correct answers from peers. But uncontrolled, it causes **disagreement collapse** — premature convergence on incorrect conclusions in both peer debates and centralized judging.

The DReaMAD study (arXiv:2503.16814) shows this convergence happens fast: despite initial divergence, agents converge on a shared line of reasoning **after just the first round**, even when the initial consensus is incorrect. Multi-agent debate "often serves as an **amplifier of bias** rather than a correction mechanism."

For code review, these findings are directly relevant. When a challenge agent sees the original agent's reasoning about a code finding, the strong prior expectation is sycophantic confirmation. The empirical research strongly suggests that **challenge agents should see only the finding and the code, not the original reasoning** — accepting the cost of potentially re-deriving context in exchange for independent judgment.

## Production systems favor pipelines over debate

The major production code review systems have converged on architectures that avoid open-ended debate entirely, instead using structured pipelines with verification steps and centralized aggregation.

**Qodo 2.0** employs a three-layer architecture: an orchestrator selects specialist agents (bugs, security, performance, architecture), specialists run in parallel with dedicated context windows, and a **Judge Agent** consolidates findings. The Judge explicitly resolves conflicts — when a performance agent flags an optimization opportunity that contradicts the architecture agent's preference for clarity, the Judge "weighs these tradeoffs rather than surfacing contradictory feedback." The Judge also applies team-specific calibration (a fast-shipping startup gets different tolerances than a regulated fintech), deduplicates findings seen from multiple angles, and enforces a **high confidence-and-relevance threshold**. Qodo reports an **F1 score of 60.1%** on their benchmark of 580 defects across 100 production PRs, with an extended multi-agent configuration reaching 79% precision and 60% recall.

**Anthropic's Code Review** (launched March 2026) dispatches multiple specialized agents in parallel — logic validation, security analysis, regression testing, pattern compliance, and performance — scaling agent count with PR complexity. The architecture is a **multi-stage pipeline**, not a debate: parallel analysis → verification step (testing hypotheses against actual code behavior) → final aggregation agent (deduplication, severity ranking). There is no voting or debate mechanism. The key innovation is the verification step, which provides cross-agent validation by checking findings against code behavior before surfacing them. Internal metrics show substantive review comments jumped from **16% to 54%** of PRs, with a **<1% false positive rate** (measured as developer dismissals). The system deliberately focuses on logic errors only, excluding style issues — a design choice that dramatically reduces false positives.

**CodeRabbit** uses a hybrid pipeline with deterministic analysis first (40+ linters, code graph construction) and agentic loops inserted where judgment is needed. A **verification agent** checks and grounds review findings using shell and Python scripts — "comments come with receipts." There is no multi-agent consensus mechanism; uncertainty is managed through deterministic verification and learnable preferences that adapt based on developer dismissal patterns. CodeRabbit reports approximately **44% bug detection** and an 8% initial false positive rate that decreases over time.

**Calimero AI's open-source Code Reviewer** (github.com/calimero-network/ai-code-reviewer) is the only production-oriented tool implementing explicit consensus-based scoring: findings are weighted by **severity × agreement** across 2–5 agents. Identical findings from multiple agents get higher consensus scores. However, the project is very early-stage with no published effectiveness data.

**Open Code Review** (github.com/spencermarx/open-code-review) is the only open-source tool implementing an explicit **debate mechanism** for code review. After parallel reviews by customizable reviewer personas (28 built-in, including named engineering personas), a structured discourse phase has reviewers examine each other's findings with AGREE and CHALLENGE responses. Previous rounds are preserved with shared context across multi-round reviews. This is the closest implementation to a true challenge round in production code review.

## Disagreement signals matter more than disagreement resolution

The "Wisdom and Delusion of LLM Ensembles" paper (Vallecillos-Ruiz et al., arXiv:2510.21513) studies 10 LLMs across 5 families on code generation and repair benchmarks, finding that the **theoretical upper bound** for ensemble performance is **83% above the best single model** — confirming substantial complementarity even within model families. But the paper reveals a "popularity trap": consensus-based strategies (majority voting, naive selection) amplify common but incorrect outputs and can perform **worse than the best single model**. A diversity-based selection strategy using CodeBLEU similarity achieves **up to 95% of theoretical potential**, even in two-model ensembles. The key insight: when models disagree on code, selecting the most *different* solution — the minority specialist — often yields the correct answer.

This finding maps directly onto DeepMind's debate safety research. Irving et al.'s foundational "AI Safety via Debate" (2018) established the complexity-theoretic argument: debate can verify PSPACE-complete questions with polynomial-time judges, versus only NP for direct evaluation. Kenton et al.'s large-scale empirical study (NeurIPS 2024, ~5 million model calls) confirmed that **debate consistently outperforms consultancy** (a single AI arguing for one answer) across all 9 tasks tested. But they also found debate "is susceptible to inconsequential features such as which debater had the last word" — a position bias that mirrors the anchoring effects documented in the broader literature.

CodeX-Verify (Harvard/Noumenon Labs, arXiv:2511.16708) provides the most relevant quantitative data for code verification specifically. Using 4 specialized agents (Correctness, Security, Performance, Style) with **inter-agent correlation of only ρ = 0.05–0.25**, the system achieves 76.1% bug detection. The diminishing returns are precisely quantified: agents 2, 3, and 4 add **+14.9, +13.5, and +11.2 percentage points** respectively — following a proven submodularity theorem. The best 2-agent combination (Correctness + Performance) reaches 79.3% accuracy, suggesting 2–3 agents may be the cost-optimal configuration. Notably, CodeX-Verify uses OR-based aggregation (any agent flagging = flag) rather than majority voting, effectively giving each specialist **veto power** in its domain — accepting a 50% false positive rate as the cost of maximum sensitivity.

Position bias research confirms that **order effects are real** in multi-agent evaluation. Shi et al.'s "Judging the Judges" study (ACL 2025, 150,000+ evaluation instances) found position preferences of **60–75%** in some configurations, with the quality gap between solutions as the dominant driver: similar-quality findings produce maximum position bias. The Microsoft Magentic Marketplace found "severe first-proposal bias" creating **10–30x advantages for response speed over quality**. Position swapping (evaluating twice with reversed order) reduces bias to near-neutral and is the most robust structural mitigation.

## Empirical evidence reveals critical gaps in the literature

The most important finding across this research landscape is the **vast gap between theoretical debate/voting research and practical code review applications**. Several specific areas lack empirical evidence entirely.

**False negative risk is unmeasured.** No published study directly measures true bugs found by one agent and then voted down by a challenge mechanism in code review. The closest evidence comes from the Knight-Knave-Spy debate study (arXiv:2511.07784), where majority opinion suppresses independent correction less than 5% of the time for weak agents. The "Debate or Vote" martingale proof implies that correct minority findings can be voted down just as easily as incorrect ones — but this hasn't been tested in defect-finding contexts. The Human-AI Synergy study (arXiv:2603.15911, analyzing 278,790 code review conversations) found that human reviewers accept only **16.7% of AI defect suggestions** — meaning roughly 83% are rejected, though this conflates false positives with valid findings that humans chose to ignore.

**Cost-benefit analysis is primitive.** CodeX-Verify's diminishing-returns quantification is the best available: 4 agents run in 148ms versus 260ms sequential, with mathematically proven submodular gains. Anthropic's reviews take ~20 minutes at **$15–25 per review**. But no published study compares the dollar cost of multi-agent verification against the dollar cost of bugs reaching production.

**Confidence recalibration shows promise but lacks code-specific validation.** AlignVQA demonstrates that debate combined with calibration-aware loss reduces Expected Calibration Error from **0.375 to 0.098** in medical VQA. Collaborative Calibration (arXiv:2404.09127) shows training-free multi-agent deliberation improves calibration on reasoning benchmarks. But the martingale result implies that debate rounds alone don't systematically improve belief accuracy — calibration improvements require explicit mechanisms beyond naive debate.

**Weighted voting for code review is entirely theoretical.** The NousResearch hermes-agent project proposes security-weighted voting (GitHub issue #412) but hasn't implemented it. The MDPI adversarial framework found that **overly skewed weights toward a single model reduce consistency** — balanced contributions are essential. No system has published results comparing weighted versus uniform voting for code findings.

## A concrete design for challenge rounds in multi-agent code review

Based on the empirical evidence, here is a recommended architecture that maximizes the benefits of multi-agent disagreement while avoiding the documented failure modes:

- **Phase 1 — Independent parallel analysis.** Deploy 3–4 specialist agents (correctness, security, performance, and optionally architecture) with isolated context windows. Each agent sees only the diff, relevant codebase context, and its domain-specific prompt. Inter-agent correlation should be low (target ρ < 0.25, per CodeX-Verify). Agents produce findings with structured severity ratings and confidence scores.

- **Phase 2 — Disagreement detection, not debate.** Rather than having agents debate, a lightweight aggregation step identifies three categories: (a) consensus findings (multiple agents flag the same issue), (b) singleton findings (only one agent flags), and (c) contradictions (agents make conflicting claims about the same code). Consensus findings are scored by severity × agreement count (following Calimero's approach). Singleton findings from domain specialists in their domain of expertise pass through without challenge. Contradictions trigger Phase 3.

- **Phase 3 — Blind challenge for contradictions only.** When agents contradict each other, a fresh challenge agent receives *only* the conflicting claims and the relevant code — never the original reasoning (to avoid sycophancy, per the identity bias research). The challenge agent evaluates each claim independently and either resolves the contradiction or flags it for human escalation. Apply Response Anonymization (strip agent identity labels) per the Wisconsin-Madison findings.

- **Phase 4 — Deterministic verification.** Following Anthropic's and CodeRabbit's approach, surviving findings undergo deterministic verification where possible: testing hypotheses against actual code behavior, running linters, checking type signatures, or executing test cases. This is the most effective false-positive filter in production systems.

- **Phase 5 — Escalation rules.** Security findings survive unless explicitly contradicted with verified evidence (asymmetric treatment reflecting the asymmetric cost of false negatives in security). Unresolvable contradictions surface both perspectives to the human reviewer with explicit framing of the disagreement. Findings that survive both challenge and verification get a calibrated confidence boost.

The critical design choices in this architecture reflect empirical findings: avoiding open-ended debate (martingale property), preventing context sharing between challenger and original agent (sycophancy research), using verification over voting where possible (Anthropic's <1% FP rate), and treating disagreement as a signal for difficulty rather than a problem to eliminate through consensus (the "Wisdom and Delusion" diversity insight). The expected cost is 1–2 additional agent calls per contradiction, applied only to the small fraction of findings that actually conflict — far cheaper than challenging every finding, and targeted at exactly the cases where challenge provides the most value.

## Conclusion

The multi-agent code review field sits at an inflection point between two competing insights. The martingale result proves that naive debate doesn't systematically improve correctness — yet ensemble diversity demonstrably catches bugs that no single agent finds, with theoretical gains of up to 83% over the best individual model. The resolution lies in **how** disagreement is processed. Production systems have already moved past debate toward verification pipelines, centralized judges, and deterministic checking. The research suggests they're right to do so: sycophancy corrupts debate in 18 of 20 tested configurations, performance degrades with additional debate rounds in many settings, and the "popularity trap" means majority voting can underperform single-model solutions on code tasks.

The most underexploited insight is that **disagreement itself is the signal**. Rather than resolving disagreement through consensus, the optimal strategy is to detect it, preserve it, and route it to the appropriate resolution mechanism — blind challenge for contradictions, deterministic verification for testable claims, and human escalation for genuinely ambiguous cases. The field's most critical gap is the complete absence of empirical data on false negatives — true bugs found by one agent but suppressed by challenge or voting. Until that data exists, any challenge mechanism should err on the side of surfacing rather than suppressing, with asymmetric treatment favoring security and correctness findings over style and optimization suggestions.
